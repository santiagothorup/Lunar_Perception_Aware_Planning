\section*{Abstract}

Autonomous lunar rovers operate without satellite navigation and must therefore localize against on-board sensing alone. Visual-inertial SLAM systems used for this purpose accumulate drift roughly proportional to distance traveled --- at the multi-kilometer scales planned for upcoming lunar surface missions, the resulting localization error can reach tens of meters unless actively bounded. Loop closure, the SLAM mechanism by which re-encountering a previously mapped region corrects accumulated drift, is the principal software-only mitigation, but it requires the rover's mission plan to physically revisit feature-rich, visually distinctive terrain. Heritage slope-aware planners optimize for traversability and energy rather than perceptibility, and rarely produce trajectories that fire loop closures incidentally. We present a perception-aware global planner that engineers loop closures through two composable mechanisms. First, the A* edge cost is augmented with DEM-derived perception terms --- a shadow-avoidance penalty and a traversability-safety penalty --- that route the rover through sunlit, feature-stable corridors while staying clear of unsafe rocky regions. Second, an online keyframe-revisit detour mechanism monitors the SLAM backend's loop-closure history and, after a configurable drift budget elapses without a loop closure, splices a detour through a previously visited keyframe back into the planned tour, then resumes. The detour target is driveable by construction (the rover already went there) and loop-closure-eligible by construction (the backend already stored a keyframe there). On a six-sub-goal perimeter tour in the Stanford NavLab Lunar Autonomy Challenge simulator, the proposed planner achieves SLAM RMSE of 0.14~m on a 139~m trajectory --- a 4.16$\times$ reduction in absolute localization error and a 5.55$\times$ reduction in per-meter drift relative to a slope-aware A* baseline, despite a 35\% longer path. Twenty of the 54 loop closures fired during the run bind late-mission keyframes back to the first 10\% of the trajectory, providing the global drift correction the heritage planner cannot.


\section{Introduction}

Autonomous lunar rovers operating on the south polar region of the Moon cannot rely on satellite navigation: GNSS does not reach the Moon, and a dedicated lunar GNSS infrastructure does not yet exist. Localization is derived instead from on-board sensing --- typically a fusion of stereo visual odometry, an inertial measurement unit, and wheel encoders --- closed into a SLAM pose graph that grows monotonically with mission duration~\citep{campos2021orbslam3, qin2018vins}.

This work uses the Stanford NavLab Lunar Autonomy Challenge (LAC) framework~\citep{stanford_navlab_lac} as its test environment and reference perception stack. The LAC pipeline --- stereo SuperPoint~\citep{detone2018superpoint} features matched by LightGlue~\citep{lindenberger2023lightglue}, accumulated as relative-pose constraints in a GTSAM~\citep{dellaert2012gtsam} factor graph and optimized by Levenberg--Marquardt --- is used here \emph{unmodified}. The contributions of this paper lie strictly in the \emph{global path planner} and an \emph{online loop-closure detour mechanism} that sits on top of NavLab's perception and SLAM stack; no changes are made to the feature frontend, the pose-graph backend, the loop-closure trigger, or the local arc-based controller.

Visual-inertial SLAM systems are known to drift at 0.5--1\% of distance traveled in well-instrumented terrestrial conditions; on the lunar surface --- where regolith is texture-poor in shadow, the sun grazes the horizon at altitude below 2$^\circ$, and rocks are sparse but visually rich --- drift is empirically worse. NASA's Volatiles Investigating Polar Exploration Rover (VIPER)~\citep{nasa2022viper} plans a multi-kilometer south-polar traverse; even sub-1\% drift accumulates to hundreds of meters of localization error, well beyond the sub-meter tolerances required for sample collection and infrastructure emplacement.

The standard mitigation is \emph{loop closure}: when the rover re-observes a previously mapped region, the SLAM backend matches the current imagery against an earlier keyframe and adds a relative-pose constraint that pulls accumulated drift back toward the original anchor. Loop closures are the only mechanism in our pipeline that \emph{bounds} SLAM error globally rather than letting it grow monotonically with distance. They are not free: the rover must physically revisit terrain that is visually distinctive enough to produce successful feature matches and geometrically close enough to a stored keyframe for the backend's proximity-based candidate search to find it. A mission plan that never revisits its own trail will fire few or no loop closures, and its localization will drift unbounded.

This work asks: \emph{can the global path planner be made loop-closure aware?} The dominant lunar planner archetype --- slope-aware A* over a digital elevation model (DEM)~\citep{stanford_aa278_hw3} --- optimizes for traversability and energy. It cannot predict whether a planned leg will leave the rover visually blind, and it produces trajectories that fire loop closures only incidentally. We propose to instead treat the global planner itself as a \emph{loop-closure engineering tool}: route the rover through cells the DEM predicts to be sunlit and visually stable, and, when SLAM has been drifting for too long without a loop closure, actively detour through a past keyframe to engineer one.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we introduce a perception-aware A* edge cost that augments the heritage slope penalty with a DEM-derived shadow-avoidance term and a traversability-safety term, and show that the shadow term is the most robust of three perception-cost candidates we evaluate. Second, we propose an online \emph{keyframe-revisit} detour mechanism that, when triggered by elapsed-steps-since-last-loop-closure, splices a detour through a previously visited keyframe into the planned tour. We contrast this with a \emph{tight-anchor} alternative that targets DEM-predicted feature-rich cells, and show that the keyframe-revisit tactic substantially outperforms it. Third, we report a 3$\times$3 ablation matrix on the LAC simulator demonstrating that the composite \emph{Shadow~$+$~Safety} cost combined with the \emph{Keyframe-Revisit} detour achieves 0.14~m SLAM RMSE on a 139~m tour --- a 5.55$\times$ per-meter drift reduction over the slope-aware A* baseline.

A central mechanistic finding sharpens the contribution: 20 of the 54 loop closures fired by the winning variant bind late-mission keyframes back to the first 10\% of the trajectory, near the GTSAM prior pose. These cross-trajectory bindings --- not mid-trajectory loop closures --- are what enables \emph{global} drift correction. All code, configurations, and figures are available at the project repository~\citep{thorup2026repo}.


\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Visual-inertial SLAM and loop closure.} Modern visual-inertial SLAM systems such as ORB-SLAM3~\citep{campos2021orbslam3} share a common architecture: a tightly coupled visual-inertial frontend feeds a pose-graph backend optimized by iSAM2 or GTSAM~\citep{dellaert2012gtsam}. The LAC reference SLAM follows this archetype, with loop-closure detection performed as a \emph{geometric} proximity check on the SLAM-estimated pose. Appearance-based loop-closure search using bag-of-words descriptors~\citep{galvez2012dbow2} is the prevailing alternative, robust to large SLAM drift, but requires an additional descriptor pipeline we did not modify. The implication for our work is structural: the geometric trigger \emph{cannot detect} a true revisit once accumulated drift exceeds its proximity tolerance. Our planner-side mechanism is designed to operate within this constraint rather than fix it.

\textbf{Active and perception-aware SLAM.} \citet{carrillo2012} formalize active SLAM as a problem of choosing control actions to maximize information gain about the map and pose. Subsequent work generalizes to next-best-view planning~\citep{bircher2016receding} and to perception-aware planners that augment traversability cost with a feature-richness reward computed from imagery or a learned predictor~\citep{costante2018perception, zhang2018perception}. These priors typically operate either \emph{locally} (next-best-view) or over a \emph{pre-mapped} environment. We differ in that our feature-density prediction is computed \emph{a priori} from a DEM with no prior visual observation; this is appropriate for lunar planning because orbital DEMs derived from sources such as the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter~\citep{robinson2010lro} are the only a-priori knowledge available.

\textbf{Lunar rover localization and planning.} Drift is widely recognized as the dominant localization risk for lunar surface missions~\citep{petersen2022lunar}, with mitigations historically focused on stop-and-localize against orbital imagery or operator-in-the-loop waypoint adjustment. To our knowledge, none treats the global planner itself as a loop-closure engineering tool. The slope-aware A* baseline used here, derived from the Stanford AA278 Homework~3 Problem~4 formulation~\citep{stanford_aa278_hw3}, is representative of operational lunar planners.


\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Environment and Assumptions}
We use the Stanford NavLab Lunar Autonomy Challenge (LAC) simulator as the test environment. The rover navigates a bounded 2D ground plane on which a ground-truth digital elevation model $Z : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ and a known sun direction (fixed across the mission) are available a priori. The rover's pose is estimated by an on-board SLAM system whose state is a sequence of keyframe poses $\{X_i\}$ in a factor graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ containing VO odometry factors and, opportunistically, loop-closure factors. The SLAM estimate $\hat{X}_i$ may differ from the true pose $X_i^*$ by an accumulated drift $\|\hat{X}_i - X_i^*\|_2$ that grows with mission duration.

The mission is a \emph{tour}: an ordered sequence of $M$ sub-goals $\mathcal{G}_{\text{tour}} = (g_1, \dots, g_M)$ with $g_k \in \mathbb{R}^2$, visited in order from a spawn pose $x_s$. The closed-loop tour case sets $g_1 = g_M$ so the rover returns to the start.

\subsection{Global Planning Objective}
The global planner produces a waypoint sequence $W = (w_0, \dots, w_N)$ with $w_0 = x_s$ and $w_N = g_M$, generated by an A* graph search between each consecutive sub-goal pair and concatenated at a fixed spacing. A local controller handles safe steering between waypoints. The A* edge cost between adjacent cells $n_1, n_2$ takes the general form

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:edge_cost}
\begin{aligned}
c(n_1, n_2) = d \cdot \Big(
    & 1
    + \alpha \, f_{\text{slope}}(n_2) \\
    & + \beta \, f_{\rho}(n_2)
    + w \, f_{\text{trav}}(n_2) \\
    & + s \, f_{\text{shadow}}(n_2)
\Big),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $d$ is the Euclidean distance between cells; $f_{\text{slope}}$ is a slope penalty derived from DEM gradients with a hard infeasibility cutoff; $f_{\rho}$ is a \emph{look-ahead} feature-density reward derived from a DEM-based perception predictor (defined in Sec.~\ref{sec:approach}); $f_{\text{trav}}$ is a roughness-based traversability penalty; and $f_{\text{shadow}}$ is a shadow-avoidance penalty. The relative weights $(\alpha, \beta, w, s)$ define a family of cost variants we study. The multiplier $(1 + \cdots)$ is always $\ge 1$, so the Euclidean distance heuristic remains admissible.

\subsection{Loop Closure and the Localization Metric}
On each new keyframe, the SLAM backend adds a VO odometry factor to the previous keyframe and searches for loop-closure candidates among earlier keyframes whose SLAM-estimated pose is geometrically close to the current estimate. For each candidate, a feature-matching step decides whether to add a relative-pose loop-closure factor to the graph, which is then re-optimized. The crucial structural property of this trigger --- the one that motivates our planner-side mechanism --- is that the candidate search uses the \emph{SLAM-estimated} pose, not ground truth: once accumulated drift exceeds the trigger's proximity tolerance, the backend cannot detect a true revisit even if the rover is physically standing on top of a previously mapped cell.

The end-of-mission localization error is the RMSE between SLAM and ground-truth trajectories,

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rmse}
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \|\hat{X}_i - X_i^*\|_2^2}.
\end{equation}

We also report per-meter drift $\mathrm{RMSE}/L_{\text{path}}$, which normalizes for variants whose ground-truth trajectory lengths differ; absolute RMSE on a short path is not directly comparable to RMSE on a longer one.


\section{Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{DEM-Derived Perception Predictor}
\label{sec:predictor}

From the DEM we compute a per-cell roughness field $r(n)$ (local elevation standard deviation) and a binary shadow mask $\mathrm{shadow}(n) \in \{0, 1\}$ obtained by ray-casting each cell toward the sun and checking for occlusion. The base predictor combines them as

\begin{equation}
\rho_{\text{full}}(n) = r(n) \cdot \big(1 - \mathrm{shadow}(n)\big),
\end{equation}

and is normalized per-map to give $\rho_{\text{norm}}(n) \in [0, 1]$. The intuition: a cell is predicted to be \emph{feature-rich} if it is both geometrically rough (rocks and topography produce visual texture) and sunlit (shadowed cells produce no features regardless of underlying texture).

A subtlety drives the implementation. The relevant quantity is not the predicted feature density of the cell the rover is \emph{on}, but the predicted feature density of the cell the camera is \emph{looking at}. We therefore evaluate $\rho$ as a \emph{look-ahead} predictor: at cell $n_2$ entered from $n_1$, average $\rho_{\text{norm}}$ at a small set of forward distances along the entry direction $\hat{u} = (n_2 - n_1)/\|n_2 - n_1\|$. Figure~\ref{fig:perception_field} shows the two predictor inputs on the evaluation preset.

\textbf{Phase~0 validation.} Before evaluating the planner end-to-end, we validated the predictor offline: a safe collector agent traversed a serpentine raster on the LAC simulator, and for each captured stereo frame we compared the SLAM frontend's observed feature count against the candidate predictor values. The strongest correlate was the look-ahead form of $\rho$, while the pose-cell form \emph{anti-correlated} with features. The look-ahead Pearson coefficient was positive but \emph{borderline} in strength --- enough to confirm that the predictor captures real structure, but not strong enough to drive a global cost alone. This observation motivated both the traversability-safety penalty (to prevent the planner from steering into rocky cells the local controller cannot navigate) and the shadow-only variant (a simpler cost that does not depend on the borderline-strength roughness predictor) introduced below.

\subsection{Three Global-Cost Configurations}
\label{sec:variants}

Equation~\eqref{eq:edge_cost} parameterizes a family of A* costs through the perception weights $(\beta, w, s)$, with the slope penalty held at the heritage baseline value. We evaluate three qualitatively distinct settings. The \emph{Baseline} disables every perception term and recovers the heritage slope-aware A*. The \emph{High Feature} variant routes toward look-ahead-predicted feature-rich cells, paired with a traversability-safety penalty to keep the rover off the rocks that co-occur with high-$\rho$ predictions. The \emph{Shadow Avoidance} variant drops the $\rho$ reward entirely and relies only on the shadow indicator --- \emph{prefer sunlit cells}; a composite \emph{Shadow~$+$~Safety} setting adds the traversability term back in and constitutes the headline winner reported in Sec.~\ref{sec:results}. \subsection{Online Loop-Closure Detour}
\label{sec:detour}

The second mechanism is an online detour triggered when SLAM has been drifting for too long without a loop closure. A SLAM-state provider closure is injected into the planner; on each step it returns the current pose-graph index $i$, the list of loop closures fired so far, and the set $\mathcal{K}_{\text{eligible}}$ of past keyframe positions outside the backend's exclude window. The detour fires when the elapsed steps since the last loop closure $\Delta t_{\text{LC}}$ exceeds a threshold $T_{\text{LC}}$. A target $\tau \in \mathcal{K}_{\text{eligible}}$ is then chosen from a proximity band around the current SLAM pose to minimize the added path length

\begin{equation}
\Delta L = \|p \!\to\! \tau\| + \|\tau \!\to\! g_k\| - \|p \!\to\! g_k\|,
\end{equation}

where $p$ is the current pose and $g_k$ is the next un-reached sub-goal. The planner \emph{replaces} the remaining waypoint list with a freshly planned A* path $p \to \tau \to g_k \to \cdots \to g_M$; a sub-goal-index offset preserves the global tour counter across re-plans. The detour is marked \emph{verified-success} when the SLAM backend fires a loop closure whose anchor keyframe lies near $\tau$; otherwise it is timed out, the target is blacklisted for that leg, and the rover resumes the tour. A per-leg attempt cap prevents unbounded detour loops. Algorithm~\ref{alg:detour} summarizes the mechanism in symbolic form; the specific threshold values are deferred to the Results section.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\small
\caption{Online Keyframe-Revisit Detour}
\label{alg:detour}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require pose $p$, sub-goal $g_k$, SLAM state $\{i,\,\{(a_l, c_l)\},\,\mathcal{K}_{\text{eligible}}\}$; parameters $T_{\text{LC}}, T_{\max}, N_{\max}$
\State $\Delta t_{\text{LC}} \gets i - c_{|\text{LC}|}$
\If{detour active with target $\tau$}
    \If{new LC fired near $\tau$} mark success; clear active
    \ElsIf{step $-$ start $> T_{\max}$} blacklist $\tau$; clear active
    \EndIf
    \State \Return
\EndIf
\If{$\Delta t_{\text{LC}} < T_{\text{LC}}$ \textbf{or} attempts$_{\text{leg}} \ge N_{\max}$} \State \Return \EndIf
\State $\mathcal{C} \gets \{X_j \in \mathcal{K}_{\text{eligible}} : X_j \text{ in proximity band of } p\} \setminus \text{blacklist}$
\If{$\mathcal{C} = \emptyset$} \State \Return \EndIf
\State $\tau \gets \arg\min_{X_j \in \mathcal{C}} \Delta L_j$
\State $W \gets \mathrm{A^*}(p \!\to\! \tau \!\to\! g_k \!\to\! \cdots \!\to\! g_M)$
\State Replace remaining waypoints with $W$; activate detour
\State attempts$_{\text{leg}} \gets$ attempts$_{\text{leg}} + 1$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\emph{Alternative: Tight Anchor.} We also evaluate a \emph{Tight Anchor} variant that, at planner construction, pre-computes a small set of anchor cells from local maxima of $\rho_{\text{norm}}$ filtered for slope, roughness, and rock-density feasibility, and uses those anchors as detour targets instead of past keyframes. The two tactics differ in a structurally important way: Tight Anchor \emph{predicts} good revisit locations from the DEM, while KF Revisit \emph{guarantees} a driveable, LC-eligible target by construction (the rover already went there, the backend already stored a keyframe). Section~\ref{sec:results} shows that this difference is decisive.

\subsection{Evaluation Mission}
To expose meaningful drift while keeping mission length comparable across variants, the evaluation mission is a closed six-sub-goal perimeter tour: the rover departs the spawn pose, visits five corner-style sub-goals along the operational-area perimeter (with one mid-route pivot to skirt impassable terrain), and returns to the start. The closed-loop structure guarantees one end-of-mission revisit opportunity by construction; the perimeter routing forces a long enough trajectory between revisits that loop-closure-free dead reckoning would accumulate substantial drift.


\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\emph{Setup.} All evaluations run on the LAC simulator (Unreal~Engine~4, headless Xvfb rendering) on a single preset representative of lunar south-polar terrain. The mission is the six-sub-goal perimeter tour described in Sec.~\ref{sec:approach}; the local controller and the SLAM stack are the unmodified NavLab LAC submission. Reported metrics are SLAM RMSE against ground truth, per-meter drift $\mathrm{RMSE}/L_{\text{path}}$, ground-truth path length, sub-goals reached, stationary fraction, and the number of loop closures fired. A run is valid if the agent finalized cleanly and reached at least four of six sub-goals.

\subsection{Phase 0: DEM Predictor Validation}
\label{sec:phase0}

Before evaluating the planner end-to-end, we tested whether the DEM-derived predictor $\rho$ correlates with observed visual-feature density. A safe collector agent traversed a serpentine raster across the operational area, capturing $n = 10{,}565$ stereo frames; for each frame we computed the SuperPoint feature count and the LightGlue stereo-match count, and compared them against four candidate predictor values --- pose-cell $\rho$ and look-ahead $\rho$ at three forward distances. To control for spatial autocorrelation in the raster, we report both the raw Pearson coefficient and a block-bootstrapped 95\% confidence interval over an i.i.d.\ subsample ($n_{\text{iid}} = 121$).

Table~\ref{tab:phase0} summarizes the strongest correlations at the best-performing look-ahead distance ($d = 1.5$~m). Three observations dominate. First, the look-ahead form of $\rho$ correlates \emph{positively} with feature and match counts (Pearson $0.21$--$0.25$; Spearman in the same direction), while the pose-cell form \emph{anti-correlates} (Pearson $-0.14$ to $-0.26$): the predictor must be evaluated at the cell the camera is \emph{looking at}, not the cell the rover is \emph{on}. Second, the strongest correlate is the LightGlue stereo-match count, suggesting that the predictor tracks downstream usable feature density rather than raw frontend keypoint counts. Third, the magnitude of the correlation is \emph{borderline-positive} (Pearson $\sim 0.25$) --- enough to be statistically distinguishable from zero (lower CI bound above $0.11$) but not enough to drive a global cost on its own, which directly motivates the safety-paired and shadow-only variants studied below. Figure~\ref{fig:phase0} shows the correlation sweep across look-ahead distances and the matched-stereo response by roughness quartile.

\subsection{Why This Combination Works: Mechanism Analysis}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The winner combines three mechanisms, none of which is sufficient alone. \emph{(a) The shadow-aware cost keeps SLAM accurate}: the winning trajectory spends only 15.3\% of its path-distance in shadow versus the baseline's 57.5\%, so SLAM-estimated pose stays within $\texttt{LC\_DIST\_THRESHOLD} = 1$~m of ground truth long enough that the geometric LC trigger keeps working rather than going blind. \emph{(b) The traversability-safety penalty prevents rocky-cell stalls}: without $w = 4$, the Shadow-only variant (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, row ``Shadow Avoidance / none'') reaches only four of six sub-goals and accumulates 3.07~m of RMSE; adding $w = 4$ produces the cleanest mobility profile of any tested variant. \emph{(c) The KF Revisit detour actively engineers loop closures}: with $T_{\text{LC}} = 400$, nine detours fire over the run and 54 loop closures result.

The structurally important finding: \textbf{20 of the 54 LCs in the winning variant bind back to keyframes in the first 10\% of the mission}, near the GTSAM prior pose at $X_0$, and a further 28 fire in the last 20\% (during the return-to-start leg $g_5 \to g_6$). Cross-trajectory LCs that bind late keyframes to keyframes anchored by the pose-graph prior are what enables \emph{global} drift correction; mid-trajectory LCs only reduce local drift. The combination of mechanisms (a) and (c) above is what produces these cross-trajectory bindings.

\subsection{Full 3$\times$3 Ablation Matrix}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reports the complete 3$\times$3 ablation. Three observations: (i)~\emph{the baseline global cost is fragile under aggressive detours} --- slope-only A*~$+$~Tight Anchor diverges catastrophically (RMSE 130.8~m), and slope-only~$+$~KF Revisit reproduces a downstream LAC-stack sensor-dropout failure at step 894; (ii)~\emph{Tight Anchor is environment-sensitive} --- it works with High Feature global cost ($w \ge 8$), times out under Shadow Avoidance, and diverges under the baseline, because its anchor cells co-occur with rocky cells the rover cannot reach without a heavy roughness penalty; (iii)~\emph{KF Revisit is the most robust detour mechanism} --- it works with all three global costs (0.90, 0.74, 0.14~m) because it targets keyframes the rover has \emph{already visited}, driveable by construction.

\subsection{Failure Modes}

Three failure modes shape the matrix. UE4 collision meshes invisible to the DEM (the lunar lander at the origin, smaller crater meshes elsewhere) deadlocked a previous-tour Tight Anchor variant on a sub-median-roughness cell with zero DEM-recorded rocks; we removed the central $(0, 0)$ waypoint as a result. Simulator non-determinism near rocks produced RMSE 0.58~m on one baseline run and 9.08~m on another with identical offline-planner output; we report the best-completed baseline as the reference. An LAC-stack camera-frame dropout terminated \texttt{kf\_revisit\_base} with a $\texttt{KeyError}$ at step 894.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We demonstrated that path-planner-side loop-closure engineering is a viable mechanism for reducing SLAM drift on lunar-rover tour-style missions. On the LAC simulator, a composite \emph{Shadow~$+$~Safety} global cost combined with an online \emph{Keyframe-Revisit} detour achieves SLAM RMSE of 0.14~m on a 139.4~m tour --- a 4.16$\times$ absolute RMSE reduction and a 5.55$\times$ per-meter drift reduction over the slope-aware A* baseline, with no modifications to the perception or SLAM stack. The structural lesson is that 20 of the winning variant's 54 loop closures bind late-mission keyframes back to the first 10\% of the trajectory, providing the cross-trajectory drift correction the heritage baseline cannot. \emph{Tight Anchor collects loop closures; KF Revisit causes them.}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

The published baseline is the best-completed run (we observed simulator non-determinism); results are reported on a single preset; and DEM-driven planning is fundamentally blind to UE4 collision meshes not represented in the elevation model. The most impactful next steps are an appearance-based loop-closure trigger to extend the operational envelope of the detour mechanism beyond the backend's geometric proximity window, multi-run statistical evaluation, and re-validation of the predictor on LRO-derived terrain at the kilometer scale where heritage-planner drift dominates.
